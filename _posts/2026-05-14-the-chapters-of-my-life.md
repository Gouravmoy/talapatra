## Chapter 1 – The Ending

I am dying. In a few days, I suppose. What a relief that would be. Living was not as good as advertised anyway. Good riddance, I say. A shitty end to a shitty life, I suppose.

I have Stage 4 stomach cancer. The doctors guess it is because of the unsanitary living conditions and unhygienic water I drank in Tihar jail during my fifteen years of off-and-on incarceration. Most days, the pain is unbearable. Sometimes it is so sharp that I just keel over onto the floor. During those times, I can barely breathe. I must grunt like a wounded animal just to force my wrecked body to keep breathing. How nice it will be not to have to strain every muscle in my body just to breathe. Yes, I am looking forward to death.

I don’t really want to talk about why I was in jail. All I had to say, I said during my short two-week trial, when I was declared guilty by everyone even before it started. No one paid any heed to my words then, and I don’t care about it now. By the end of those two weeks, I was pronounced guilty and sentenced to life in prison. I was twenty-two years old that day.

Imagine, a life sentence. For an unforgivable decision and a few moments of insanity, a punishment for life.

After many years of incarceration, I finally came to understand what they went through. I feel remorse for what I did that night. But after the incident, they carried on with their lives. Yes, they would be scared and traumatized for the rest of their lives. But they lived. Every day, the memory of the incident probably became more distant in their minds. They got married and had kids. They breathed fresh air.

For me, it was the stagnant air of prison. Every day was a constant reminder of what I did and how I could never fix it. Never make it better. Never know what it meant to be a free person ever again.

For many years, I tried to accept my fate. Sometimes it got better; sometimes worse. Months would go by in my prison cell. I caught quite a few diseases during my incarceration. They would take months to heal and always left me in a weakened state. There would be long stretches of silence. Sometimes I would try speaking out loud just to confirm if I still had my voice anymore. If there was any sound left in the world.

During my time in prison, I barely had any contact with my family. I could hardly blame them. I was born into an upper-middle-class household, with my father being a highly respected senior government official. After my conviction, I met my mother and sister a couple of times, and my father just once. They did their best to support me during my short trial, and afterward they had to live with the associated horrors of my conviction. They were shunned by society.

## Chapter 2 – Running for My Life

My mother got sick around the time I had been incarcerated for eight years. My father used his official connections to get me fourteen days’ furlough from prison so I could see her.

Getting out of prison was hard. I knew I had to use every moment of the next fourteen days to live before coming back. I had an anxiety attack the night before my release. I could hardly breathe, and my bed was drenched with sweat.

A car was waiting at the prison gate. It was a six-hour drive to my home, and our family driver took me there. He barely spoke to me, though he remained courteous. I didn’t mind. During the journey, as I entered my hometown, I had another anxiety attack, but I managed. I reached home and saw my family. They embraced me. There were tears all around.

That first night, I sat beside my mother as she slept. I slept on the ground and was woken the next day by my sister. For a few moments, I was startled, as I was used to waking up alone. My whole body stiffened. I was shaking. I controlled myself with a great deal of effort.

On the tenth day, my father told me to run away.

He said he had everything arranged. He could get me a new driver’s license and identification. He knew someone who could arrange hotel stays to help me disappear. He had not told anyone else in the family about this. He knew it was going to ruin him and his career. But still, he was willing to do it for me.

I said yes.

Maybe it was a moment of weakness, selfishness, or cowardice—I don’t know—but I took him up on his offer.

Over the next couple of days, I met a man who explained the escape plan. He gave me all my new identification and told me which train to catch, which hotels to stay in, and how to avoid detection. He told me to keep changing hotels and move toward the south of India, where it would be harder for anyone to recognize me.

## Chapter 3 – Life in Shadows

I ran on the thirteenth day of my furlough. At every moment of the journey—at the train station, at my first hotel, and then at the second—I thought someone was going to recognize me. Even though I had grown a full beard, my photos had been splashed across all the national dailies during my trial eight years earlier.

But I did not get caught. I barely left the hotel rooms I stayed in. I kept moving from one city to another. One day, I found an old newspaper lying in the hotel where I was staying. On page three, an old photo of mine taken during my college admission was printed with the headline: “*Prisoner jumps furlough, high official father disgraced and suspended.*”

My father had known this would happen. But he had made the decision.

After three months of staying in hotels, I finally found a small one-bedroom apartment in the Kottayam district of Kerala. The room was in a dilapidated condition, and the walls had severe water seepage issues. That was probably the reason the owner was willing to rent it out to an unknown North Indian fellow who did not have a job.

In Kottayam, I kept a low profile. I mostly stayed in my room. During the time I spent there, I focused on learning Malayalam, the language spoken in Kerala. It was difficult, learning a new language, but I needed to blend in with the locals so as not to arouse suspicion.

A year passed in Kottayam, during which I had become a model tenant in my apartment complex. My neighbors knew me as Rajat Kumar from Bihar, a man who had come to Kottayam for business. A quiet man who helped the older residents in the neighborhood with grocery shopping and also spoke fluent Malayalam. They were very impressed by how quickly I had picked up the local language.

I decided to appear for the Kerala State banking examination. It’s surprising how a few months of freedom can transform the mind of a condemned man but I had began to tentatively dream about my future. I was afraid that my new identification would not withstand proper government scrutiny and that I would be caught. But I applied for it nevertheless. I put all my attention into preparing for the exam. I liked having a goal and working toward it. I cleared the exam. I was so happy that day. My heart yearned to talk to my parents and give them the news, but I knew I couldn’t. I had promised my father not to contact the family after I jumped furlough.

## Chapter 4 – Storm Is Coming

The day before I was due to join my posting at the Kottayam branch of Kerala Bank, I had another anxiety attack. I was gripped with debilitating fear. Would anyone recognize me? Would I be caught? Would I have to go back to jail?

But my joining the office went smoothly. No one looked at me strangely, and no suspicion was raised about my identity. Its amazing how much people will look past if you are an outsider who speaks their language.

Throughout the seven years I worked at the bank, I was the model employee. Always on time and a hard worker. It helped that I had no social life. I helped others with their work but never took credit for it. I did not want to be in the limelight. My fellow employees liked me for this. I did my work and kept my head down. After office I was always happy to go back to my apartment and read books. I never invited guests into my house. I did not want to push my luck.

Eventually, I started feeling more confident. I was still cautious, but happy. I had a roof over my head and a room full of books. For a foolish moment, I thought life would continue like this. Me, my work, and my books.

I suppose a sense of complacency crept into me. Once you have been in prison for some time, you develop a renewed appreciation for places. You see them with different eyes. It’s hard not to be optimistic in Kottayam. It’s a beautiful city with a lot of greenery. The people there are very kind and accepting of outsiders, provided you can speak their language. Maybe a city where so much of its youth emigrates to work in the Middle East is happy to welcome any new blood.

I never saw the curveball coming.

## Chapter 5 – Navigating from the Back Seat

Her name was Amu. She worked in my office as a bank teller. She was beautiful, a hard worker, and always on good terms with the rest of the office. I interacted with her sometimes during office functions.

I am not sure why, but she became my friend despite my intention not to become close to anyone in the office. Later, she would say that she was drawn to me, fascinated by me. Fascinated by my silence and by the distance I maintained from everyone. These qualities, instead of keeping her away from me, attracted her toward me. I did not know this then, but I noticed that she was always there. Always near me, coming over and talking to me during break times and office functions. She was like a persistent pet who refused to leave your side. I started becoming comfortable around her. She was very easy to talk to. We discussed a wide range of topics, from office work to politics, from history to her love for knitting. I was always careful not to reveal anything from my past to her.

After two years of friendship, one day after lunch, she proposed to me. She wanted to take our friendship one step further. She wanted a relationship. I was taken aback and asked for time to decide. I deliberated for days, thinking of all the things that could go wrong. Should I tell her about my past? No, I had already decided I was never going to reveal my past to anyone. But then, what could come of a relationship built on a lie? I also thought of the possibilities of life with Amu. How it would change, having love in my life. Having someone who would keep me company. Because of one reckless night of mistakes, did I not deserve any happiness for the rest of my life?

With some reluctance, I said yes. The next few months were the best of my life. We went on many dates. I rediscovered the foodie in me. I looked up the best food places in the city, and we would go there together. We went to the movies as well. She had a bike—a maroon Honda Activa. She would come to my apartment and wait by the gate. I would go down the stairs, enduring the curiosity and mischievous smiles of my neighbors. Then Amu and I would roam around Kottayam. After years in captivity, the feeling of movement, of wind rushing to meet your face and flowing through your hair—the feeling is indescribable. I was the navigator with Google Maps in the back seat, and she was the driver. For those few months, I almost forgot my past and, for the first time, lived in the present.

## Chapter 6 – Pain

On a few of our outings, when I had spicy food, I started having pain in my lower back the next day. In the beginning, it was mild, but it gradually became worse. I reduced eating oily food, but the pain became persistent. I was afraid to go to the doctor for a diagnosis lest I be caught. So I took whatever over-the-counter pain medication I could find. I never told Amu about it. I did not want her to worry.

It happened on a Friday evening. After office, Amu and I had planned a dinner date. As soon as I got back from work, I started feeling unwell. The pain in my back was so bad I could barely move from my bed. It took all my strength just to stand up and get some water, but I crumpled back onto the bed. I also started running a high fever. When Amu came to the apartment gate on her bike and called me, I could not answer. The phone kept ringing just out of my reach.

I heard footsteps and then a knock on my door. It was Amu. It took all the strength I could muster to drag myself to the door and open it. As soon as I did, I crumpled to the floor. Amu had to drag me back to bed. I lay there half delirious with pain and fever. She stayed with me the whole night, caring for me. I do not remember much of what happened that night. I had a fever of 105, and I had taken pain medication.

When I woke in the morning, she was still beside me. Her eyes were red. She had probably been crying. I had no idea why. She stayed the whole weekend taking care of me. My fever went down, and so did the back pain. But over the weekend, Amu became increasingly distant. She avoided my eyes. On Sunday afternoon, she said she needed to go home to change her clothes and collect a few things. She said she would be back. I was finally able to get out of bed, clean my room, and take a bath.

Amu came back at 8 p.m. with some homemade idiyappam and kadala curry. It was very tasty, especially since I had not eaten much in the past few days. We ate together. She seemed withdrawn, but I assumed it was exhaustion after taking care of me for two straight days. After clearing the food, we sat in silence. I went to bed and sat resting my back against the wall. She came and joined me. We sat side by side, looking at each other without speaking.

And then she kissed me as if her life depended on it. We made love that night. It was my first time since the “incident” nine years earlier. That night, I surrendered my body and possibly my soul to her. That night, she seemed to accept everything about me. We barely spoke and let our bodies do all the communication. When she looked into my eyes, I was afraid she was looking into my soul, and I could not stop her.

Toward daybreak, we were exhausted and fell asleep holding each other. She held me more tightly than usual. As I slept, I felt a few drops of tears fall onto my chest. She was weeping.

## Chapter 7 – The Beginning of the End

When I woke up, she was gone. Little did I know that it was the last time I would see her. I tried calling her, but she did not pick up. I did not know what to do. A deep sense of foreboding took over me.

The police came to my door at 10 p.m. They banged on it, calling me by my real name. I froze where I was sitting. No one had called me by that name in the past seven years. I started shivering. It took all my strength to get up and open the door. I was sure my legs would not support me when I saw the police, but somehow they did. As they cuffed me, my neighbors stood huddled by the door of my apartment, peering inside and whispering. As the police marched me out, I could not help but look back at the small solitary apartment with its water-soaked walls that had been my refuge for so many happy years. My books, my small chair and table, my bed.

It had happened on Friday night, when I was down with a high fever and half delirious from the pain medication I had taken for my back pain. Initially, my mutterings had been incoherent. But I had said things that Amu picked up. I had started muttering in my mother tongue. I had said my real name and, apparently, begged for forgiveness from my parents. It did not take her long to search for my name, and the internet spat out my whole life story and all its gory details.

Why she informed the police, I do not know. When she made up her mind to tell them, I do not know either. There is a part of me that wishes I could say I do not blame her. I wish she had given us a chance. I wish we could have gone on more dates, discovered more food, taken many more long rides on her Activa, and felt the free wind blowing through my hair as I sat behind her on that bike.

But there was another part of me that was also relieved. The part of me that knew I would never have been able to live with her after what she had read about me. After what she knew I had done and been accused of. Why would she ever tie her innocent life in Kottayam to a criminal like me?

I was back in Tihar jail. This time, they did not let me leave. I spent the next ten years there without interruption. During that time, to keep myself occupied, I started reading law books and completed a long-distance bachelor’s degree in law through an open university. It was also during one of the excruciating incidents of back and stomach pain that I was diagnosed with stomach cancer. It was Stage 2 then, and the doctor gave me five years.

## Chapter 8 – When I sleep

I applied for compassionate release. I had already served more than eighteen years in prison, and I was dying. This time, I fought my own case and won. After nineteen years, I was a free man.

I went to my parents’ home. My mother had passed away, and my father had retired. I was back in the house and in the room I had grown up in. I practiced probate law from a home office, helping people draft family wills and estate documents. I liked it because I did not have to go to court. I rarely went out of the house or met people, though my sister often visited when she was back from abroad.

In the last few months, the pain has grown worse, and I had to shut down my practice. I can barely eat nowadays. The stomach cancer is Stage 4 now. The doctors only give me pain medication. I sleep on a hospital bed—a fifty-three-year-old man with a broken body but an unbroken mind.

When I am awake, I feel agitated and anxious. I keep wondering what my life could have been if not for that one fateful night thirty years ago. Of all the pain and misery I brought upon my family.

But when I sleep, all I can see is Amu. Our long rides sitting on the back of the Activa, almost feel the wind in my hair. I see the beautiful city of Kottayam and my books.

I finally feel peace.
